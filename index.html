Subtheme: Transit-Oriented Development

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How does population density intersect with transit availability?

    To answer this borad question, I chose to explore some more specific sub-questions:

    • How is the population distributed in the Boston area?
    • How is the transportation network distributed?
    • How many people live in transit deserts in the Boston area? Specifically, are there areas with high population density and few transit options?

    Motivation: The background reading Short Explainer on Transit-Oriented Development emphasized clustering jobs, amenities, and services around transportation hubs. This made me curious about Boston's current urban layout and how well its population is served by transit.

  2. How does income intersect with transit availability?

    Motivation: The article from A1 Reckoning with Boston’s Towers of Wealth discussed how luxury housing developers were required to build affordable housing, but chose to do so a mile and a half away. This sparked my curiosity about whether luxury housing tends to be closer to better transit options, while affordable housing is pushed farther out, with fewer transit options.

  3. Is there an income difference between areas with rapid transit access versus those served only by bus services?

    Motivation: From personal experience searching for apartments in Cambridge and assisting friends moving to the Boston area, I’ve found that transportation is often the key factor when choosing a place to live. People generally prefer the T over buses due to its reliability. This led me to wonder if neighborhoods with rapid transit access (like the T) tend to have higher income levels compared to those only served by bus transit.

Discoveries & Insights

Summary

In this data exploration activity, I examined Census data on population, household size, and household income. I also looked at MBTA route information to compare income and population distributions against transit availability. Several key takeaways emerged from the analysis:

First, the dataset appears to have some inconsistencies, or the metadata may be insufficient to properly interpret the data. Based on population sizes that don't align with easily verifiable real-world figures, I find the dataset difficult to trust. Similar discrepancies were noted in the household counts data.

Second, transit in these municipalities largely follows a hub-and-spoke model, with Boston at the center. This structure is consistent with the population distribution. While there are areas that lack rapid transit access, the bus network is extensive and provides robust coverage across these areas.

Finally, the data suggests that lower-income areas in Boston correlate with higher levels of transit availability, particularly rapid transit. This finding challenges the initial assumption I made in Question 3, that areas closer to the T would be more desirable and, therefore, dominated by higher-income residents. One possible explanation is that individuals with lower incomes are more reliant on public transit, while wealthier people may have the means to afford cars.
